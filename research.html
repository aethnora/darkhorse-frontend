The Search for an Information Edge

In financial markets, the idea that prices reflect all public information is a foundational concept. Yet, persistent academic and empirical evidence reveals pockets where this efficiency breaks down. This report focuses on two such groups: corporate insiders and members of the U.S. Congress, whose trades can offer valuable signals to outside investors.1

Corporate Insiders

Why Executive Trades Are a Powerful Signal

The most fundamental conclusion from decades of financial research is that, on average, stocks purchased by corporate insiders subsequently outperform the market. When top executives—CEOs, CFOs, and directors—invest their own capital into their company's stock, it's a profound vote of confidence in its future prospects. This isn't just theory; it's a persistent market anomaly backed by extensive data. While insiders might sell for many reasons (diversification, liquidity), they typically buy for one reason: they believe the price is going up.

The Opportunist's Edge

A pivotal Harvard study distinguished between "routine" traders (e.g., selling on a schedule) and "opportunistic" traders who act on perceived mispricing. A portfolio mimicking only opportunistic traders generated abnormal returns of 0.82% per month, while routine traders' returns were "essentially zero."11 Our alerts help you spot the opportunists.

The Ultimate Tell: Late Filings

Rigorous academic research has shattered the myth that late Form 4 filings are clerical errors. They are often a deliberate, highly profitable strategy to conceal trades made just before price-moving news.

Late-filed trades were found to earn 2.10% over three months, compared to just 0.39% for timely filings—a staggering difference of 171 basis points.6
